How To Use This Dashboard

Workflow

Use Compare first. It shows the review-priority queue, the underdog pivots worth considering, where the candidates actually diverge, and the tiebreaker implications.

Confidence tiers

Locks are stable favorites. Leans still favor one side. Toss-ups are true decision points. Volatile games have wider uncertainty even when a favorite exists.

Interpret the dots and bars

Dots are posterior mean win probabilities and bars are posterior intervals. The more important the round and the wider the uncertainty, the higher a game rises in the review queue.

What this shows

The highest-priority games to revisit, ordered by how important and uncertain they are. Each row now shows the full posterior credible interval for the favorite.

How to use it

Start here. Read from top to bottom and only spend manual review time on the rows that genuinely change your bracket choices. Use the interval to see whether the edge is narrow or actually stable.

Why it matters

This is the fastest way to separate cosmetic uncertainty from decisions that can meaningfully move your pool outcome.

Underlying math

Rows are sorted by review priority = round weight x (underdog win probability + interval width). A game rises when it is both later in the bracket and less settled.

Upset Opportunity Board

What this shows

The underdog picks with the best mix of plausible win probability and downstream payoff. Each row shows the underdog posterior mean and the derived credible interval for that upset path.

How to use it

Use this after the ranked board when you want to decide whether a risky upset is worth taking or leaving as a favorite. The uncertainty track shows whether the underdog has a real chance or just a thin tail.

Why it matters

Not every upset helps. This board surfaces the swings that can actually change the shape of the bracket instead of rewarding random chaos.

Underlying math

Rows are sorted by leverage = round weight x underdog win probability x (1 + interval width). A pivot ranks higher when the underdog has a real shot and the round payoff is larger.

Why it matters

A second bracket candidate is only useful if you can explain how and where it differs from the safer path.

Underlying math

This board filters to rows where the chosen winner or matchup path changes between candidates. Late-round rows are shown first because they create larger downstream payoff differences.

Championship Tiebreaker Comparison

What this shows

These charts compare the full title-game total distribution for each candidate on a shared x-axis.

How to use it

Use the recommended tiebreaker as your default entry. Only move off it if you intentionally want a different miss-distance profile from the field.

Why it matters

If two brackets look similar, the tiebreaker can still decide the pool. Shared scales make it obvious when one path pulls totals materially higher or lower.

Underlying math

The recommended tiebreaker is the rounded median of the championship total-points predictive distribution. The shaded band is the 80% interval and the bars show the probability mass over exact totals.
